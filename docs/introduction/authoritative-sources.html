Textbook References​

Core Physical AI & Embodied Intelligence Textbooks​

  1. Pfeifer, R., & Bongard, J. (2006). "How the Body Shapes the Way We Think: A New View of Intelligence." MIT Press.

    • Comprehensive overview of embodied cognition
    • Foundational concepts in morphological computation
    • Essential for understanding Physical AI principles

  2. Brooks, R. (1991). "Intelligence without representation." Artificial Intelligence, 47(1-3), 139-159.

    • Foundational paper challenging symbolic AI approaches
    • Early work on embodied intelligence
    • Critical for understanding AI layer concepts

  3. Siciliano, B., & Khatib, O. (Eds.). (2016). "Springer Handbook of Robotics." Springer.

    • Comprehensive reference for all robotics aspects
    • Detailed on mechanical, electrical, and control layers
    • Essential for technical grounding

  4. Sutton, R. S., & Barto, A. G. (2018). "Reinforcement Learning: An Introduction." MIT Press.

    • Authoritative source on RL for robotics
    • Covers learning algorithms for physical interaction
    • Critical for AI layer research

Key Academic Papers​

Physical AI & Embodied Intelligence​

  1. Doncieux, S., Boucheny, D., & Meyer, J. A. (2010). "Continual and developmental learning in embodied agents." Frontiers in neurorobotics, 4, 20.

    • Developmental robotics approach to embodied learning
    • Relevant to sensorimotor learning concepts

  2. Coradeschi, S., Loutfi, A., & Wrede, B. (2013). "A short introduction to cognitive robotics." Foundations and Trends in Robotics, 2(3), 203-261.

    • Cognitive robotics and physical AI foundations
    • Integration of perception, action, and cognition

  3. Pfeifer, R., Lungarella, M., & Iida, F. (2007). "Self-organization, embodiment, and biologically inspired robotics." Science, 318(5853), 1088-1093.

    • Self-organization in embodied systems
    • Biological inspiration for humanoid design

Humanoid Robotics​

  1. Kajita, S., Kanehiro, F., Kaneko, K., Yokoi, K., & Hirukawa, H. (2003). "The 3D linear inverted pendulum mode: a simple modeling for a biped walking pattern generation." Proceedings 2003 IEEE/RSJ International Conference on Intelligent Robots and Systems, 2, 239-246.

    • Fundamental walking pattern generation for humanoid robots
    • Essential for control layer understanding

  2. Hirai, K., Hirose, M., Haikawa, Y., & Takenaka, T. (1998). "The development of Honda humanoid robot." Proceedings of the 1998 IEEE International Conference on Robotics and Automation, 2, 1321-1326.

    • Historical development of humanoid robotics
    • Mechanical and control insights

  3. Khatib, O., Park, H. J., Park, I. W., Bessonnet, G., & Morel, G. (2009). "The common component architecture for developing dynamically balanced whole-body humanoid behaviors." 2009 9th IEEE-RAS International Conference on Humanoid Robots, 64-70.

    • Whole-body control for humanoid systems
    • Critical for control layer research

Simulation and Real-World Transfer​

  1. Koenemann, J., Del Prete, A., Hentout, A., Mastronicola, N., Shin, P. S., & Wieber, P. B. (2015). "Real-time motion generation of humanoid robots using a task-based inverse kinematics formulation." 2015 IEEE-RAS 15th International Conference on Humanoid Robots, 936-941.

    • Simulation and real-world validation
    • Task-based control approaches

  2. Sadeghi, A., & Levine, S. (2017). "CAD2RL: Real single-image flight without a single real image." arXiv preprint arXiv:1611.04208.

    • Simulation-to-reality transfer techniques
    • Deep learning in simulation contexts

Natural Language and Social Interaction​

  1. Lopes, M., Melo, F. S., & Montesano, L. (2012). "Affordance learning for multiple objects through exploration." 2012 IEEE/RSJ International Conference on Intelligent Robots and Systems, 4812-4817.

    • Learning affordances through interaction
    • Critical for perception-action integration

  2. Breazeal, C. (2003). "Toward sociable robots." Robotics and autonomous systems, 42(3-4), 167-175.

    • Social interaction in robots
    • Human-robot interaction principles

Verification Criteria for Sources​

Academic Rigor​

  • Peer-reviewed publications preferred
  • Reproducible results and methods
  • Clear methodology and evaluation

Recency Considerations​

  • Prioritize recent developments in rapidly evolving fields
  • Include foundational papers where appropriate
  • Balance historical importance with current relevance

Credibility Indicators​

  • Authors' affiliation with recognized institutions
  • Citations and impact in the field
  • Replication and validation by other researchers
