Source Evaluation & Filtering

Date: December 15, 2025
Module: Introduction to Physical AI & Humanoid Robotics
Task: Task 1.3 - Evaluate & Filter Sources
Status: In Progress

Evaluation Framework​

Criteria for Source Assessment​

Academic Credibility (Weight: 30%)

  • Peer-reviewed status
  • Journal/conference reputation
  • Author institutional affiliation
  • Citation count and impact

Relevance to Topic (Weight: 25%)

  • Direct applicability to Physical AI
  • Alignment with humanoid robotics concepts
  • Coverage of mechanical, electrical, control, or AI layers
  • Suitability for graduate-level audience

Recency (Weight: 20%)

  • Publication date relative to rapid field advancement
  • Balance between foundational and recent work
  • Availability of updated versions or follow-up studies

Methodology Quality (Weight: 15%)

  • Reproducibility of results
  • Rigorous experimental design
  • Validated evaluation metrics
  • Clear limitations acknowledged

Accessibility (Weight: 10%)

  • Availability to target audience
  • Clarity of presentation
  • Adequate technical depth without being overly complex

Recency Assessment​

Foundational Sources (Over 5 years old, but still relevant)​

  • Brooks (1991) - Still foundational in Physical AI
  • Hirai et al. (1998) - Historical importance in humanoid development
  • Pfeifer & Bongard (2006) - Foundational for embodied cognition

Current Sources (Within 5 years)​

  • Koenemann et al. (2015) - Recent control techniques
  • Sadeghi & Levine (2017) - Contemporary simulation methods
  • Most conference papers from 2018-2025

Balance Justification​

  • Foundational concepts remain valid in Physical AI
  • Recent technical developments incorporated
  • Mix provides both theoretical grounding and current practices

Relevance Validation​

Core Relevance to Physical AI & Humanoid Robotics​

  • All selected sources address either theory, implementation, or both
  • Coverage spans mechanical, electrical, control, and AI layers
  • Suitable for graduate-level understanding

Audience Appropriateness​

  • Sources suitable for readers with basic ML/programming background
  • Technical depth appropriate for advanced study
  • Clear connection to practical implementation

Accessibility Considerations​

Available to Academic Audience​

  • Most sources available through academic databases
  • Standard textbooks accessible through libraries
  • Open access papers included where possible

Complementarity Check​

  • Sources cover different aspects without significant overlap
  • Range from foundational to cutting-edge
  • International perspectives included

Quality Assurance for Selected Sources​

Peer Review Status​

  • All journal articles peer-reviewed
  • Conference papers from reputable venues
  • Books from established academic publishers

Citations & Impact​

  • High citation counts for foundational papers
  • Recent papers from active research areas
  • Authors with recognized expertise

Reproducibility​

  • Methodology clearly described in technical papers
  • Standards properly referenced
  • Experimental protocols documented
